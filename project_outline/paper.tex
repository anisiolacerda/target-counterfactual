\begin{abstract}
Variational counterfactual intervention planning selects treatment sequences by optimizing an evidence lower bound (ELBO) toward a point target outcome. In clinical practice, however, the goal is typically to bring a patient's state into an \emph{acceptable range} while maintaining \emph{safety constraints} at every intermediate step---requirements that point-target optimization does not address. We introduce \emph{reach-avoid constrained selection}, a post-hoc method that filters ELBO-ranked candidate sequences through a reach-avoid feasibility criterion: the terminal outcome must lie within a target set~$\cT$ and all intermediate outcomes must remain within a safety set~$\cS$. We prove that reach-avoid ranking is more robust to variational approximation error than point-target ELBO ranking, with an explicit bound relating the ranking preservation margin to the variational gap~$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ and soft-indicator approximation error~$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}$. We further provide a sensitivity analysis under hidden confounding, showing that the safety guarantee degrades gracefully as the Rosenbaum sensitivity parameter~$\Gamma$ increases, with non-vacuous bounds through $\Gamma = 2.0$ at all tested horizons. Empirically, on a cancer tumor growth simulator with known counterfactuals, reach-avoid constrained selection improves safety by 18--33 percentage points under moderate-to-strong confounding at modest ranking cost, Pareto-dominates soft-constraint alternatives, and transfers to all baseline models without retraining. On MIMIC-III intensive care data, the method lifts in-target diastolic blood pressure rates from 73--81\% to 99--100\%. To our knowledge, this is the first systematic safety evaluation of counterfactual treatment planners. The approach requires no model modification, no retraining, and is compatible with any variational counterfactual planner.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Individualized treatment planning from observational data is a central challenge in precision medicine~\citep{chakraborty2014dynamic, murphy2003optimal}. Given a patient's clinical history, the goal is to recommend a sequence of interventions---medications, dosages, ventilation settings---that will produce the best outcome over a planning horizon~$\tau$. Recent advances in variational latent-dynamics models have made this tractable: Variational Counterfactual Intervention Planning \citep[VCIP;][]{melnychuk2025vcip} learns a latent state-space model from observational trajectories and selects treatment sequences by optimizing an evidence lower bound (ELBO) toward a target outcome value.

However, clinical treatment planning has two features that point-target ELBO optimization does not capture:

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Range targets, not point targets.} Clinicians do not aim for a single number. They aim for an \emph{acceptable range}: diastolic blood pressure in $[60, 90]$~mmHg, tumor volume below a stage-dependent threshold, blood glucose within safe bounds. A treatment plan that overshoots the target by 1\% and one that undershoots it by 50\% receive the same ELBO penalty if they are equidistant from the point target, yet their clinical implications differ fundamentally. This limitation was explicitly identified in the VCIP peer review process, where a reviewer noted the restrictive nature of single-point targets for clinical applications and the authors acknowledged range-based targets as important future work.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Path safety, not just terminal achievement.} ELBO-based planning evaluates only the terminal outcome~$Y_{t+\tau}$. It is blind to what happens along the way. A chemotherapy regimen that produces dangerously high toxicity at step~$t{+}3$ before recovering at step~$t{+}\tau$ may rank identically to a safe regimen---both achieve the same terminal tumor volume. In clinical practice, intermediate safety violations are unacceptable: a patient whose blood pressure drops to dangerous levels during treatment has suffered harm regardless of the final reading.

These concerns arise naturally from the reach-avoid control literature~\citep{mitchell2005reach, fisac2015reach}, where the objective is to steer a dynamical system into a target set while avoiding unsafe regions throughout the trajectory. Figure~\ref{fig:concept} illustrates the distinction: ELBO-based selection picks the sequence with the best terminal loss (D), ignoring its unsafe intermediate path, while reach-avoid constrained selection picks the best ELBO among sequences with safe trajectories (A).

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Our approach.} We propose \emph{reach-avoid constrained selection}: given a set of candidate treatment sequences ranked by ELBO, we filter out sequences that fail a reach-avoid feasibility criterion and select the best ELBO among the feasible set:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ra_selection}
\ba^* = \arg\min_{\ba \in \cF_{\mathrm{RA}}} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba),
\end{equation}
where $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}} = \{\ba : Y_{t+\tau}[\ba] \in \cT \text{ and } Y_s[\ba] \in \cS \text{ for all } s = t, \ldots, t{+}\tau\}$ is the set of reach-avoid feasible sequences. This is deliberately simple: no new model, no retraining, no gradient-based planning. The method operates as a \emph{post-hoc safety filter} on any existing variational counterfactual planner.

The simplicity is a feature, not a limitation. By separating safety (via the RA filter) from quality (via ELBO ranking), we avoid the failure mode we observed when attempting to use RA scoring as a \emph{replacement} for ELBO ranking: the RA score is near-binary (sequences are either clearly feasible or clearly not), providing no discrimination among candidates. ELBO ranking and RA feasibility are complementary---each does what the other cannot. Any practitioner building a clinical decision support system would add safety checks; our contribution is the \emph{formalization} that makes this principled and the \emph{systematic evaluation} revealing when and why it matters.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{First systematic safety evaluation of counterfactual planners.} We formalize reach-avoid constrained intervention planning, separating safety from quality in a way that reveals a previously unquantified safety gap: existing planners violate clinically meaningful constraints on 15--45\% of patients under moderate confounding (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\item \textbf{Ranking robustness theory.} We prove that reach-avoid ranking is more robust to variational approximation error than point-target ELBO ranking (Theorem~\ref{thm:ranking_robustness}), with an explicit, empirically non-vacuous bound ($\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} \approx 0.09$--$0.23$) on ranking preservation (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).
\item \textbf{Sensitivity analysis under hidden confounding.} We extend the Rosenbaum sensitivity model to sequential treatments and prove that the RA safety guarantee degrades gracefully as hidden confounding increases (Theorem~\ref{thm:sensitivity}), with non-vacuous bounds through $\Gamma = 2.0$ at all tested horizons and explicit breakdown points $\Gamma_{\max} = 2.1$--$7.5$ (Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity}).
\item \textbf{Comprehensive experiments.} On the Cancer simulator, RA-constrained selection yields $+$18--33~pp safety improvement under moderate-to-strong confounding, Pareto-dominates soft-constraint alternatives, and generalizes across five baseline models without retraining. On MIMIC-III ICU data, it lifts in-target rates from 73--81\% to 99--100\% (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\item \textbf{Model-agnostic post-hoc method.} The approach requires no retraining---we show empirically that RA-aware retraining produces identical results to post-hoc filtering---and is compatible with any variational counterfactual planner (Section~\ref{sec:retraining_result}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We provide a concise overview here; Appendix~\ref{app:extended_related_work} contains a comprehensive survey with additional references.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Counterfactual outcome estimation and planning.} A growing body of work estimates individualized treatment effects from longitudinal observational data, including balanced representations~\citep{shalit2017estimating, bica2020crn}, variational approaches~\citep{louizos2017cevae}, attention-based architectures~\citep{melnychuk2022causal}, recurrent methods~\citep{lim2018forecasting, li2021gnet}, continuous-time models~\citep{seedat2022actin}, and synthetic control~\citep{qian2021synctwin}. Recent advances address hidden confounding~\citep{bica2020tsd, frauen2023sharp} and employ contrastive~\citep{kim2024causalcpc} or generative~\citep{wu2024counterfactual, pawlowski2020deep} approaches. These methods estimate outcomes for \emph{given} treatments but do not optimize over treatment sequences. VCIP~\citep{melnychuk2025vcip} extends this line to \emph{planning}: it selects intervention sequences by ELBO minimization toward a point target. Our work keeps the VCIP planning infrastructure intact and adds a safety-aware selection layer.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Dynamic treatment regimes.} The DTR literature~\citep{murphy2003optimal, chakraborty2014dynamic, hernan2020causal} formalizes sequential treatment decisions under the potential outcomes framework, with recent work on when-to-treat policies~\citep{nie2021learning} and batch constrained learning~\citep{le2019batch}. Most DTR methods learn decision rules mapping states to actions, optimizing expected outcomes over the full trajectory. In contrast, VCIP and our extension operate in a \emph{counterfactual planning} mode: given a specific patient history, they evaluate and rank complete intervention sequences. The reach-avoid formulation adds the constraint that trajectories must remain safe---a concern shared with constrained DTR work but addressed here through a distinct mechanism (post-hoc filtering rather than constrained policy learning).

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Safe and constrained sequential decision-making.} Safe reinforcement learning~\citep{garcia2015comprehensive, achiam2017constrained, tessler2019rcpo} constrains policies to satisfy safety specifications during execution, from Lyapunov-based methods~\citep{chow2018safe} to safety layers~\citep{dalal2018safe} to offline safe RL~\citep{zheng2024fisor}. These methods operate in the online or offline RL setting, requiring either environment interaction or fixed datasets of state-action trajectories. Our setting is fundamentally different: we select among candidate plans evaluated on a learned model, with no opportunity for policy-level correction. This distinction motivates a filter-based approach rather than constrained policy optimization. The \emph{reach-avoid} concept from control theory~\citep{mitchell2005reach, fisac2015reach, bansal2021deepreach} formalizes the objective of reaching a target set while avoiding unsafe regions. Chance-constrained optimization~\citep{nemirovski2006convex} and robust MDPs~\citep{iyengar2005robust} address related problems under uncertainty. We import the reach-avoid concept into the variational counterfactual planning framework, replacing the continuous reachability computation with a discrete feasibility check over candidate sequences.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Variational causal inference.} CEVAE~\citep{louizos2017cevae} pioneered VAE-based causal inference under hidden confounding; Deep SCMs~\citep{pawlowski2020deep} extended this to full counterfactual reasoning via normalizing flows. VCI~\citep{wu2025vci} derives an ELBO for individual-level counterfactual likelihoods in a static setting, introducing latent disentanglement via $D_{\KL}[q(z|y,t,x) \| q(z|y',t',x)]$. We draw on VCI's insight that latent action-sensitivity is a diagnostic for counterfactual model quality (Section~\ref{sec:e4_diagnostic}), adapting it to the sequential setting. Our diagnostic reveals that VCIP's latent space is nearly action-invariant ($D_{\KL} \approx 10^{-5}$), with action-outcome coupling residing entirely in the decoder.

\section{Background: Variational Counterfactual Intervention Planning}
\label{sec:background}

We briefly review the VCIP framework~\citep{melnychuk2025vcip} to establish notation.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Setting.} Consider a longitudinal observational dataset with patient histories $\bH_t = (X_1, A_1, Y_1, \ldots, X_t, A_t, Y_t)$, where $X_s \in \R^p$ are time-varying covariates, $A_s \in \cA$ are treatments (binary or categorical), and $Y_s \in \R^d$ are outcomes of interest. Given a patient's history $\bH_t$ up to time $t$ and a planning horizon $\tau$, the goal is to select an intervention sequence $\ba = (a_{t+1}, \ldots, a_{t+\tau}) \in \cA^\tau$ that optimizes the counterfactual outcome $Y_{t+\tau}[\ba]$.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Latent dynamics model.} VCIP posits a latent state $Z_s \in \R^{d_z}$ with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Generative transition: $p_\theta(Z_{s+1} \mid Z_s, a_s)$, modeling how latent states evolve under treatment,
    \item Emission (decoder): $p_\theta(Y_s \mid Z_s)$, mapping latent states to predicted outcomes,
    \item Variational posterior: $q_\phi(Z_{t:t+\tau} \mid \bH_t, \ba)$, approximating the intractable posterior over latent trajectories.
\end{itemize}
The model is trained by maximizing an ELBO on observed trajectories:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:elbo}
\mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi) = \E_{q_\phi}\!\left[\sum_{s=t+1}^{t+\tau} \log p_\theta(Y_s \mid Z_s)\right] - D_{\KL}\!\left[q_\phi(Z_{t:t+\tau} \mid \bH_t, \ba) \,\|\, p_\theta(Z_{t:t+\tau} \mid \bH_t, \ba)\right].
\end{equation}

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Planning by ELBO minimization.} Given a patient history $\bH_t$, VCIP generates $k$ candidate treatment sequences (via perturbation of the observed treatments) and selects the one minimizing the ELBO loss with respect to a point target $Y^*$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:vcip_selection}
\ba^*_{\mathrm{ELBO}} = \arg\min_{\ba \in \{\ba_1, \ldots, \ba_k\}} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba; \bH_t, Y^*).
\end{equation}
The ELBO loss decomposes into a reconstruction term (how well the predicted outcome matches $Y^*$) and a KL regularization term. This formulation ranks sequences by how well the variational model predicts they will achieve the point target. The key limitation is that $Y^*$ is a single point, and only the terminal step $s = t + \tau$ contributes to the ranking---intermediate outcomes are ignored.

\section{Method: Reach-Avoid Constrained Selection}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Reach-Avoid Feasibility}
\label{sec:ra_feasibility}

\begin{definition}[Reach-Avoid Event]
\label{def:ra_event}
Given a target set $\cT \subseteq \R^d$ for the terminal outcome and a safety set $\cS \subseteq \R^d$ for intermediate outcomes, the \emph{reach-avoid event} for intervention sequence $\ba$ is:
\begin{equation}
E(\ba) := \{Y_{t+\tau}[\ba] \in \cT\} \cap \bigcap_{s=t}^{t+\tau} \{Y_s[\ba] \in \cS\}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The event $E(\ba)$ requires the terminal outcome to land in the target set (\emph{reach}) while all intermediate outcomes remain in the safety set (\emph{avoid} unsafe regions). The sets $\cT$ and $\cS$ encode domain knowledge:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cancer:} $\cT = \{y : \text{tumor volume} \leq 3.0\}$ (clinically manageable stage), $\cS = \{y : \text{volume} \leq 12.0\} \cap \{y : \text{chemo dosage} \leq 5.0\}$ (no tumor explosion, no toxic chemotherapy).
    \item \textbf{ICU (MIMIC-III):} $\cT = \{y : \text{DBP} \in [60, 90] \text{ mmHg}\}$ (normotensive range), $\cS = \{y : \text{DBP} \in [40, 120] \text{ mmHg}\}$ (avoids dangerous hypo-/hypertension).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Soft Indicators}
\label{sec:soft_indicators}

To enable differentiable computation and connect to the theoretical analysis, we approximate the set membership indicators with sigmoid functions. For an upper-bounded constraint $y \leq u$:
\begin{equation}
g_u(y; \kappa) = \sigma(\kappa(u - y)),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the logistic sigmoid and $\kappa > 0$ controls hardness. For an interval constraint $y \in [\ell, u]$:
\begin{equation}
g_{[\ell, u]}(y; \kappa) = \sigma(\kappa(y - \ell)) \cdot \sigma(\kappa(u - y)).
\end{equation}
The \emph{reach-avoid score} for a candidate sequence $\ba$ is then:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ra_score}
J_{\mathrm{RA}}(\ba) = g_{\cT}(Y_{t+\tau}[\ba]) \cdot \prod_{s=t}^{t+\tau} g_{\cS}(Y_s[\ba]).
\end{equation}
A sequence is \emph{feasible} if $J_{\mathrm{RA}}(\ba) \geq \delta$ for a threshold $\delta \in (0, 1)$. In the hard-filter limit ($\kappa \to \infty$), this reduces to the indicator $\ind\{E(\ba)\}$. Our experiments show that $\kappa \geq 5$ is indistinguishable from the hard filter ($>99.9\%$ selection agreement; see Section~\ref{sec:ablation_kappa}).

\subsection{Constrained Selection}
\label{sec:constrained_selection}

Given $k$ candidate sequences $\{\ba_1, \ldots, \ba_k\}$ ranked by ELBO, the \emph{reach-avoid constrained selection} rule is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:constrained}
\ba^* = \arg\min_{\ba \in \cF_{\mathrm{RA}}} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba; \bH_t, Y^*),
\quad \text{where } \cF_{\mathrm{RA}} = \{\ba_i : J_{\mathrm{RA}}(\ba_i) \geq \delta\}.
\end{equation}
If $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}} = \emptyset$ (no feasible sequence), we fall back to unconstrained ELBO selection~\eqref{eq:vcip_selection}. The full procedure is summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:ra_selection}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Reach-Avoid Constrained Selection}
\label{alg:ra_selection}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Patient history $\bH_t$, trained model $(p_\theta, q_\phi)$, target set $\cT$, safety set $\cS$, candidates $k$, hardness $\kappa$, threshold $\delta$
\STATE Generate $k$ candidate sequences $\{\ba_1, \ldots, \ba_k\}$ via treatment perturbation
\STATE Compute $\mathrm{ELBO}(\ba_i; \bH_t, Y^*)$ for each $i$ using the variational model
\STATE Obtain outcome trajectories $\{Y_s[\ba_i]\}_{s=t}^{t+\tau}$ for each $i$ \COMMENT{simulator or model predictions}
\STATE Compute RA scores: $J_{\mathrm{RA}}(\ba_i) \gets g_\cT(Y_{t+\tau}[\ba_i]) \cdot \prod_s g_\cS(Y_s[\ba_i])$
\STATE Filter: $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}} \gets \{\ba_i : J_{\mathrm{RA}}(\ba_i) \geq \delta\}$
\IF{$\cF_{\mathrm{RA}} \neq \emptyset$}
    \RETURN $\ba^* = \arg\min_{\ba \in \cF_{\mathrm{RA}}} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba)$
\ELSE
    \RETURN $\ba^* = \arg\min_{\ba \in \{\ba_1, \ldots, \ba_k\}} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba)$ \COMMENT{fallback}
\ENDIF
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{remark}[Why hard filtering, not soft penalty]
\label{rem:hard_vs_soft}
An alternative is Lagrangian relaxation: $\min_{\ba} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba) + \lambda (1 - J_{\mathrm{RA}}(\ba))$. We found empirically that hard filtering Pareto-dominates the soft penalty at high safety levels (Section~\ref{sec:soft_vs_hard}). The key reason: a global penalty shifts rankings for \emph{all} sequences, degrading ELBO ranking quality among safe candidates. Hard filtering is surgical---it removes infeasible sequences while preserving the ELBO ranking among the feasible set. This observation also has a theoretical basis: within $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}$, the ELBO ranking is unperturbed, so the constrained selection inherits the full ranking quality of the base model.
\end{remark}

\section{Theory: Ranking Robustness}
\label{sec:theory}

We now establish that reach-avoid ranking is provably more robust to variational approximation error than point-target ELBO ranking. This result is not mathematically deep---it follows from standard properties of total variation distance applied to bounded vs.\ unbounded test functions---but it serves as a \emph{design principle}: it makes explicit \emph{why} set-membership filtering is more reliable than point-target optimization in the presence of model error, and it provides a quantitative condition (the margin inequality below) that connects directly to the gamma-sweep results in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}. The key insight is that set membership is a \emph{coarsening} of continuous outcomes: many different outcome values map to the same feasibility verdict, making the ranking less sensitive to small perturbations in the variational approximation.

\begin{assumption}[Bounded variational gap]
\label{ass:vi_gap}
For any intervention sequence $\ba$, the total variation distance between the true outcome distribution $p_\theta(\cdot \mid \ba, \bH_t)$ and the variational approximation $q_\phi(\cdot \mid \ba, \bH_t)$ is bounded:
\begin{equation}
\TV(p_\theta(\cdot \mid \ba, \bH_t),\; q_\phi(\cdot \mid \ba, \bH_t)) \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}.
\end{equation}
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded soft-indicator error]
\label{ass:soft_error}
The soft indicator $g_\cT$ approximates the true indicator $\ind_\cT$ with bounded error:
$\|g_\cT - \ind_\cT\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}(\kappa)$,
where $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}(\kappa) \to 0$ as $\kappa \to \infty$. Similarly for $g_\cS$.
\end{assumption}

Assumption~\ref{ass:vi_gap} is standard in variational inference analysis. Assumption~\ref{ass:soft_error} is constructive: for the logistic sigmoid with hardness $\kappa$, $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}(\kappa) = \sigma(0) = 0.5$ at the boundary, but this worst case occurs on a measure-zero set. We verify empirically that $\kappa \geq 5$ yields effectively zero approximation error (Section~\ref{sec:ablation_kappa}).

\begin{definition}[Reach-avoid probability]
For an intervention sequence $\ba$, the \emph{true reach-avoid probability} under the model is:
\begin{equation}
P(\ba) := \E_{p_\theta}\left[\ind\{Y_{t+\tau} \in \cT\} \cdot \prod_{s=t}^{t+\tau} \ind\{Y_s \in \cS\} \;\middle|\; \bH_t, \ba\right].
\end{equation}
The \emph{approximate reach-avoid probability} under the variational posterior is $\hat{P}(\ba)$, defined analogously with $q_\phi$ replacing $p_\theta$ and soft indicators replacing hard indicators.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Ranking robustness of reach-avoid selection]
\label{thm:ranking_robustness}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:vi_gap} and~\ref{ass:soft_error}, for any two intervention sequences $\ba_1, \ba_2$:

\textbf{(a) RA ranking preservation.} If the true reach-avoid probability margin satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ra_margin}
m_{\mathrm{RA}} := |P(\ba_1) - P(\ba_2)| > 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} + 2(\tau+1)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}(\kappa),
\end{equation}
then the approximate ranking is preserved: $\hat{P}(\ba_1) > \hat{P}(\ba_2)$ iff $P(\ba_1) > P(\ba_2)$.

\textbf{(b) Point-target ranking threshold.} For point-target ELBO ranking, let $d(\ba) = \E_{p_\theta}[\|Y_{t+\tau}[\ba] - Y^*\|^2 \mid \bH_t, \ba]$ be the true expected squared distance to target. Ranking preservation requires:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:elbo_margin}
m_{\mathrm{ELBO}} := |d(\ba_1) - d(\ba_2)| > 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} \cdot C_d,
\end{equation}
where $C_d = \sup_{\ba} \|Y_{t+\tau}[\ba] - Y^*\|^2_{L^\infty(p_\theta)}$ is the essential supremum of the squared distance over the model's outcome support.

\textbf{(c) Strict improvement.} When the boundary mass $\beta := \sup_{\ba} p_\theta(Y_{t+\tau}[\ba] \in \partial_\epsilon \cT \mid \bH_t, \ba)$ is small (most outcomes clearly inside or outside $\cT$), we have $m_{\mathrm{RA}} \gg m_{\mathrm{ELBO}} / C_d$, i.e., RA ranking is preserved for pairs where ELBO ranking would be inverted.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (full proof in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs})]
\textbf{Part (a):} The reach-avoid probability is a bounded functional ($P \in [0,1]$). By the variational characterization of total variation, $|P(\ba) - P_{q_\phi}(\ba)| \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$. The soft-indicator approximation contributes at most $(\tau+1)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}$ error (one terminal gate plus $\tau$ safety gates). Triangle inequality gives $|\hat{P}(\ba) - P(\ba)| \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} + (\tau+1)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}$. Ranking preservation follows when the margin exceeds twice this error.

\textbf{Part (b):} The squared distance $\|Y - Y^*\|^2$ can take large values, so the TV bound introduces the factor~$C_d$ (the essential supremum of the squared distance). In practice, $C_d$ is large when outcomes can be far from the target---precisely the regime where point-target ranking is most fragile.

\textbf{Part (c):} When most outcomes are unambiguously inside or outside $\cT$ (low boundary mass $\beta$), reach-avoid probabilities cluster near 0 or 1, giving large margins $m_{\mathrm{RA}} \approx 1$. Meanwhile, point distances can be nearly identical for outcomes on opposite sides of the target boundary, giving $m_{\mathrm{ELBO}} \approx 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:hard_filter_exact}
In the hard-filter limit ($\kappa \to \infty$, so $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}} = 0$), reach-avoid ranking is preserved whenever $|P(\ba_1) - P(\ba_2)| > 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$. For deterministic trajectories (as in the Cancer simulator), feasibility is binary ($P \in \{0, 1\}$), so the filter is exact whenever $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} < 0.5$.
\end{corollary}

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Empirical verification.} We estimate $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ on the Cancer simulator using rank mean absolute error as a proxy (Section~\ref{sec:ablation_eps}). We find $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} \approx 0.09$--$0.23$ across confounding strengths, with ELBO pairwise ranking preservation at 61--87\%. The bound is non-vacuous: for pairs differing in feasibility status (margin $= 1$), the condition $m > 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} \approx 0.18$--$0.46$ is easily satisfied. Notably, $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ \emph{decreases} with confounding strength $\gamma$ (the model is better calibrated when treatment effects are stronger), meaning the bound is tightest precisely where RA filtering is most valuable.

\subsection{Finite-Sample Deployment Guarantees}
\label{sec:finite_sample}

The ranking robustness results above are \emph{per-patient}: they characterize when rankings are preserved for a given patient. We now provide \emph{population-level} guarantees suitable for deployment certification (full proofs and additional results in Appendix~\ref{app:finite_sample}).

\begin{proposition}[Deployment safety guarantee]
\label{prop:deployment}
Let $\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n$ be $n$ i.i.d.\ patients from a population $\cP$. For each patient, let $\ba^*_{\mathrm{RA}}(x_i)$ be the RA-constrained selection (Algorithm~\ref{alg:ra_selection}) and let $Z_i = \ind\{E(\ba^*_{\mathrm{RA}}(x_i))\}$ indicate safety. Then with probability $\geq 1 - \delta$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:deployment_bound}
\mathrm{SR}_\cP \;\geq\; \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n - \sqrt{\frac{\ln(2/\delta)}{2n}},
\end{equation}
where $\widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i Z_i$ is the empirical safety rate and $\mathrm{SR}_\cP = \E_{x \sim \cP}[Z(x)]$ is the population rate. In particular, with oracle trajectories, $Z_i = 1$ whenever $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}(x_i) \neq \emptyset$, so $\mathrm{SR}_\cP \geq p_F$ (the population feasibility rate) with certainty.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Variance-adaptive tightening]
\label{rem:bernstein}
Since $Z_i \in \{0,1\}$ are Bernoulli with variance $\sigma^2 = \mathrm{SR}_\cP(1 - \mathrm{SR}_\cP) \leq \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n(1 - \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n)$, Bernstein's inequality yields the tighter bound:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bernstein_bound}
\mathrm{SR}_\cP \;\geq\; \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n - t_B, \quad \text{where } t_B \text{ solves } \frac{nt_B^2}{2\hat{\sigma}^2 + \tfrac{2}{3}t_B} = \ln\!\tfrac{1}{\delta},
\end{equation}
with $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n(1 - \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n)$. When $\widehat{\mathrm{SR}} \approx 1$, the variance is small and the Bernstein bound dramatically improves over Hoeffding: at $n = 100$, the deployment guarantee tightens from $\geq 80.8$--$86.2\%$ to $\geq 87.7$--$97.3\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:finite_sample}).
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Constrained selection quality]
\label{prop:quality}
Let $\eta := \sup_{\ba} |\mathrm{ELBO}(\ba) - \ell(\ba)|$ be the uniform proxy error, where $\ell$ is the true loss. Then the RA-constrained ELBO selection $\ba^*_{\mathrm{ELBO}}$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:quality_bound}
\ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{ELBO}}) \;\leq\; \ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{oracle}}) + 2\eta,
\end{equation}
where $\ba^*_{\mathrm{oracle}} = \arg\min_{\ba \in \cF_{\mathrm{RA}}} \ell(\ba)$. Total suboptimality decomposes as:
$\ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{ELBO}}) - \ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{unconstrained}}) \leq \underbrace{\ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{oracle}}) - \ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{unconstrained}})}_{\text{price of safety}} + \underbrace{2\eta}_{\text{model error}}.$
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Empirical verification.} On the Cancer simulator ($n = 100$ patients, $\gamma = 4$), the RA selector achieves empirical safety $\widehat{\mathrm{SR}} = 94.4$--$97.0\%$ across horizons (including ELBO fallback for infeasible patients). The Bernstein bound (Remark~\ref{rem:bernstein}) yields: with 95\% confidence, population safety $\geq 87.7$--$91.7\%$---substantially tighter than the Hoeffding bound ($\geq 80.8$--$83.4\%$), exploiting the near-unit safety rate. The quality bound is tight: empirical regret $\ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{ELBO}}) - \ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{oracle}})$ within $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}$ is $< 0.2\%$ of the loss range ($\ll 2\eta$), confirming that ELBO ranking quality within the feasible set is high ($\rho = 0.71$--$0.89$). Additional results (feasibility scaling, chance-constrained interpretation) are in Appendix~\ref{app:finite_sample}.


\subsection{Sensitivity to Hidden Confounding}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

The preceding results assume sequential ignorability (no unobserved confounders). We now analyze the RA filter's robustness when this assumption is violated, using the Rosenbaum sensitivity model~\citep{rosenbaum2002observational} adapted to sequential treatments.

\begin{assumption}[Bounded hidden confounding]
\label{ass:hidden_confounding}
For each step $s \in \{t+1, \ldots, t+\tau\}$, unobserved confounders $U_s$ shift treatment propensities by a bounded factor: for all histories $H_s$, treatments $a_s$, and confounders $U_s$,
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{\Gamma} \leq \frac{\pi_{\mathrm{true}}(a_s \mid H_s, U_s)}{\pi_{\mathrm{obs}}(a_s \mid H_s)} \leq \Gamma,
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma \geq 1$ parametrizes confounding strength ($\Gamma = 1$ recovers sequential ignorability).
\end{assumption}

\begin{theorem}[Sensitivity of reach-avoid safety]
\label{thm:sensitivity}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:vi_gap},~\ref{ass:soft_error}, and~\ref{ass:hidden_confounding}, for any intervention sequence $\ba$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sensitivity_bound}
|P_\Gamma(E(\ba)) - P_0(E(\ba))| \;\leq\; \psi(\Gamma, \tau) \;:=\; 1 - \left(\frac{2}{\Gamma + 1}\right)^{\!\tau},
\end{equation}
where $P_0$ is the reach-avoid probability identified under sequential ignorability and $P_\Gamma$ is the true probability under confounding level~$\Gamma$. Consequently, the population safety rate satisfies $\mathrm{SR}_\Gamma \geq \mathrm{SR}_0 - \psi(\Gamma, \tau)$, and this guarantee is non-trivial whenever $\Gamma < \Gamma_{\max}$, where
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:breakdown}
\Gamma_{\max}(\tau, \mathrm{SR}_0) = \frac{2}{(1 - \mathrm{SR}_0)^{1/\tau}} - 1.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch (full proof in Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity})]
At each step $s$, Assumption~\ref{ass:hidden_confounding} implies a $\Gamma$-bounded density ratio between the true and identified conditional outcome distributions. By the standard TV bound for bounded density ratios, the per-step conditional TV distance satisfies $\TV_s \leq (\Gamma - 1)/(\Gamma + 1)$. A sequential maximal coupling argument then yields $\TV(P_\Gamma, P_0) \leq 1 - \prod_s (1 - \TV_s) = 1 - (2/(\Gamma+1))^\tau$, which is tighter than the union bound $\tau \cdot (\Gamma-1)/(\Gamma+1)$. Since $E(\ba) \in [0,1]$, the TV bound gives Eq.~\eqref{eq:sensitivity_bound}. The breakdown point follows by setting $\mathrm{SR}_0 - \psi(\Gamma, \tau) = 0$.
\end{proof}

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Empirical sensitivity bounds.} On the Cancer simulator at $\gamma = 4$, the breakdown points are: $\Gamma_{\max} = 7.5$ at $\tau = 2$ ($\mathrm{SR}_0 = 94.4\%$), $\Gamma_{\max} = 3.4$ at $\tau = 4$ ($\mathrm{SR}_0 = 95.6\%$), and $\Gamma_{\max} = 2.1$ at $\tau = 8$ ($\mathrm{SR}_0 = 97.0\%$). At moderate hidden confounding ($\Gamma = 1.5$), the guaranteed safety rates remain $\geq 58\%$ ($\tau = 2$) and $\geq 14\%$ ($\tau = 8$). Even at $\Gamma = 2.0$ (each hidden confounder doubles the treatment odds at each step), the bound is non-vacuous through $\tau = 8$. These results confirm that RA-constrained selection provides meaningful safety guarantees under substantial hidden confounding, with robustness strongest at short horizons---precisely the regime most clinically relevant. Full sensitivity tables are in Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity}.

\vspace{-0.5em}
\begin{corollary}[Combined robustness margin]
\label{cor:combined_margin}
Under all three assumptions, the RA filter preserves pairwise rankings when the reach-avoid margin satisfies $m_{\mathrm{RA}} > 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} + 2(\tau+1)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}(\kappa) + 2\psi(\Gamma, \tau)$. This extends Theorem~\ref{thm:ranking_robustness}(a) to the confounded setting, decomposing robustness into variational, approximation, and confounding terms.
\end{corollary}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate reach-avoid constrained selection on two datasets: (1)~a cancer tumor growth simulator~\citep{bica2020cancer} where ground-truth counterfactual trajectories are available, and (2)~MIMIC-III intensive care data~\citep{johnson2016mimic} where only observed outcomes are available.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Cancer simulator.} The simulator models tumor growth under chemotherapy and radiotherapy~\citep{geng2017cancer, bica2020cancer}. Treatment assignment is confounded by a parameter $\gamma \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$: higher $\gamma$ means patients with larger tumors receive more aggressive treatment, creating stronger confounding between treatment assignment and outcomes. We use the VCIP~\citep{melnychuk2025vcip} implementation with 5 random seeds per configuration, $k = 100$ candidate sequences per patient, $n = 100$ evaluation patients, and planning horizons $\tau \in \{2, 4, 6, 8\}$.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Cancer thresholds.} Calibrated from the data distribution: target set $\cT$: tumor volume $\leq 3.0$ (below the 60th percentile, corresponding to a clinically manageable stage). Safety set $\cS$: tumor volume $\leq 12.0$ (below the 95th percentile) and chemotherapy dosage $\leq 5.0$ (below the toxic threshold) at all intermediate steps.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{MIMIC-III.} We use the MIMIC-Extract pipeline~\citep{wang2020mimic} to extract 34,472 ICU patients with hourly measurements from the MIMIC-III database~\citep{johnson2016mimic}. The outcome is diastolic blood pressure (DBP). Target set: DBP $\in [60, 90]$~mmHg (normotensive range). Safety set: DBP $\in [40, 120]$~mmHg at all steps (avoids dangerous hypo-/hypertension). We train VCIP models with 5 seeds and evaluate with $\tau \in \{2, 3, 4, 5\}$.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare five counterfactual estimation models, each evaluated with and without RA-constrained selection: VCIP~\citep{melnychuk2025vcip}, CRN~\citep{bica2020crn}, Causal Transformer (CT)~\citep{melnychuk2022causal}, RMSN~\citep{lim2018forecasting}, and ACTIN~\citep{seedat2022actin}.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Metrics.} (1)~\emph{GRP}: ground-truth rank percentile of the selected sequence among $k$ candidates (higher = better; available on Cancer only). (2)~\emph{Top-1 accuracy}: fraction of patients for whom the selected sequence is the ground-truth best. (3)~\emph{Safety rate}: fraction where all intermediate safety constraints hold. (4)~\emph{In-target rate}: fraction where the terminal outcome lies in $\cT$. (5)~\emph{Feasibility}: fraction of candidate sequences passing the RA filter.

\subsection{Hard Filter vs.\ Soft Constraint (Constrained RL Analogue)}
\label{sec:soft_vs_hard}

We compare hard filtering (Eq.~\ref{eq:constrained}) against Lagrangian relaxation: $\ba^* = \arg\min_{\ba} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba) + \lambda(1 - J_{\mathrm{RA}}(\ba))$ for $\lambda \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50\}$. This Lagrangian formulation is the direct analogue of constrained policy optimization methods from safe RL (e.g., CPO~\citep{achiam2017constrained}, RCPO~\citep{tessler2019rcpo}), which minimize a reward objective subject to cost constraints by converting them to penalty terms via Lagrange multipliers.

At $\gamma = 4$ and comparable safety (${\sim}86\%$), hard filtering achieves 32.0\% Top-1 vs.\ soft constraint ($\lambda = 50$) at 25.9\%. The hard filter Pareto-dominates across the full safety--quality frontier: for any target safety level, it achieves equal or better Top-1 accuracy. The mechanism is straightforward: the soft penalty globally shifts all ELBO rankings, degrading ranking quality even among safe sequences. Hard filtering preserves the original ELBO ranking within $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}$, affecting only the \emph{boundary} between feasible and infeasible candidates. This Pareto-dominance over the Lagrangian baseline suggests that, in the counterfactual planning setting, hard post-hoc filtering is preferable to the penalty-based approaches common in constrained RL.


\subsection{Ablations}
\label{sec:ablations}

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Target set size sensitivity.}
\label{sec:ablation_target}
The method offers a smooth, continuously tunable trade-off (Table~\ref{tab:target_size}, Appendix~\ref{app:ablation_tables}): narrow targets ($\cT_u = 1.5$) yield $+$27~pp safety at $\tau = 8$ but only 1.7\% feasibility; broad targets ($\cT_u = 10$) become near-no-ops. Our moderate default ($\cT_u = 3.0$) balances safety gain ($+$9--23~pp) with feasibility (10--54\%).

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Candidate pool expansion ($k$).}
\label{sec:ablation_k}
Feasibility at $\gamma{=}4$, $\tau{=}8$ is 10.3\% with $k{=}100$ under moderate thresholds (Table~\ref{tab:gamma_sweep}). With the per-candidate feasibility rate $p$, increasing $k$ gives $\mathbb{E}[\text{feasible}] = k \cdot p$, so $k{=}500$ yields $\sim$52 feasible candidates and $k{=}1000$ yields $\sim$103. We verify this scaling empirically (Table~\ref{tab:k_expansion}, Appendix~\ref{app:ablation_tables}): $|\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}|$ scales linearly from 70 to 710 as $k$ grows from 100 to 1000, with safety and in-target rates stable at $\sim$91\%. The computational cost scales linearly ($\sim$1.5h for $k{=}500$ vs.\ $\sim$18min for $k{=}100$ per seed).

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Sigmoid hardness $\kappa$.}
\label{sec:ablation_kappa}
For $\kappa \geq 5$, soft indicator selection agrees with the hard filter at $> 99.9\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:kappa}, Appendix~\ref{app:ablation_tables}). This validates Assumption~\ref{ass:soft_error}: $\varepsilon_{\text{soft}}(\kappa) \approx 0$ for all practical purposes.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Reach-only vs.\ reach-avoid decomposition.}
The target constraint (reach) drives most of the in-target improvement: 85\% $\to$ 96--99\% (Table~\ref{tab:decomp}, Appendix~\ref{app:ablation_tables}). The safety constraint (avoid) adds incremental value at longer horizons where trajectories pass through unsafe intermediate states. Both components are justified: reach provides the primary clinical benefit, avoid provides the formal path-safety guarantee.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{$\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ estimation and bound verification.}
\label{sec:ablation_eps}
We estimate $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ using rank MAE as a proxy (Table~\ref{tab:eps_vi}, Appendix~\ref{app:ablation_tables}; formal justification in Appendix~\ref{app:eps_proxy}). The variational gap decreases with $\gamma$ ($\hat{\varepsilon}_{\mathrm{VI}} = 0.23$ at $\gamma{=}1$ to $0.11$ at $\gamma{=}4$), meaning the Theorem~\ref{thm:ranking_robustness} bound is tightest where RA filtering is most valuable. ELBO pairwise ranking preservation is 61--87\%, and the bound is non-vacuous: for feasibility-differing pairs ($m = 1$), $m > 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ is easily satisfied.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Midpoint-baseline comparison.}
An \emph{oracle} midpoint ranker using \emph{ground-truth} terminal values achieves worse Top-1 than ELBO (58.2\% vs.\ 75.8\% at $\tau = 8$) because midpoint distance $\neq$ ground-truth loss (Appendix~\ref{app:midpoint}). RA-constrained ELBO matches the oracle's in-target rate ($\sim$94--97\%) without oracle information, while additionally enforcing path safety.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Intermediate prediction quality.}
VCIP's per-step predictions $\hat{Y}_s$ show near-zero Spearman correlation with simulator ground-truth across individuals ($\rho \approx 0$ at all steps), confirming vanilla VCIP is not calibrated for intermediate predictions. We quantify this further in the oracle-vs-model comparison below.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Oracle vs.\ model-predicted trajectories.}
To understand the dependency on oracle trajectory access, we apply RA filtering using \emph{model-predicted} cancer volumes (from the decoder at each ELBO step) instead of simulator ground truth. On Cancer, model-predicted feasibility exceeds 98\% at all gammas---meaning the filter rarely excludes any candidate---because the decoder's output range is compressed relative to true outcome diversity (model range: $[-0.4, 3.9]$~cm$^3$ vs.\ oracle range: $[0, 35]$~cm$^3$ at $\gamma = 4$). Per-patient Spearman correlations between oracle and model-predicted terminal cancer volumes are weakly \emph{negative} ($\rho \approx -0.36$ at $\gamma = 4$), indicating the decoder's trajectory predictions are not only imprecise but inversely ranked.

This reveals that the Cancer safety improvements in Table~\ref{tab:gamma_sweep} depend on oracle trajectory access. In contrast, on MIMIC-III the model's prediction range ($[48, 68]$~mmHg) straddles the target boundary ($60$~mmHg), giving model-based filtering genuine discriminating power (22--33\% feasibility). This asymmetry is a key finding: RA filtering is most effective when the model's prediction variance spans the constraint boundaries---a condition that holds on MIMIC but not on Cancer with the current decoder. Future work on trajectory calibration or conformal prediction could close this gap.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{When does RA help most?} The benefit scales with confounding strength (Table~\ref{tab:gamma_sweep}): at $\gamma = 1$, ELBO already selects safe plans; at $\gamma \geq 3$, RA filtering provides 12--33~pp safety improvement---most valuable where planning is most challenging.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Failure taxonomy.} We categorize unsafe ELBO selections into three modes (Appendix~\ref{app:failure_taxonomy}): (A)~\emph{aggressive overshoot}---high treatment intensity but tumor exceeds target; (B)~\emph{conservative undershoot}---insufficient treatment, tumor grows uncontrolled; (C)~\emph{toxic path}---terminal outcome is acceptable but intermediate states violate safety. At $\gamma{=}4$, mode~B dominates at short horizons (100\% of failures at $\tau{=}2$) while mode~C grows at longer horizons (44\% at $\tau{=}8$), confirming that path safety becomes critical for multi-step planning. Most patients (54--65/100) are ``sometimes unsafe'' across seeds---the danger is distributed, not concentrated in a few outliers---indicating that safety filtering is broadly needed rather than a correction for rare edge cases.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{The cost of safety.} The Top-1 cost (1--15~pp at $\gamma = 4$) is broadly distributed across patients (82/100 affected; Appendix~\ref{app:heterogeneity}), reflecting a fundamental property of hard constraints rather than a correctable estimation error.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Separation of concerns.} Using RA scoring as a \emph{replacement} for ELBO ranking fails (RA Top-1 $\approx 1\%$): RA scores are near-binary. ELBO ranking and RA feasibility are complementary.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Toward clinical guardrails.} The post-hoc filtering paradigm we propose is an instance of \emph{guardrails} for AI-driven treatment planning, analogous to shielding in safe RL~\citep{alshiekh2018shielding} and rejection sampling in RLHF~\citep{bai2022constitutional}: rather than training the model to be safe, we add an external safety check on its outputs. This separation has practical deployment advantages: safety thresholds can be adjusted by clinicians without retraining the model, and the same base model can operate under different constraint profiles for different patient populations or clinical contexts.

\vspace{-0.8em}
\paragraph{Limitations and future work.} (1)~$\cT$ and $\cS$ require domain expertise (Table~\ref{tab:target_size} shows robustness). (2)~On MIMIC-III, counterfactuals are unobservable; our outcome correlation and guideline concordance analyses (Appendix~\ref{app:outcome_correlation}) provide indirect evidence of clinical alignment (78--92\% treatment agreement with good-outcome patients; 97\% avoidance of unnecessary vasopressor escalation), but formal clinician evaluation would further strengthen the evidence. (3)~Feasibility drops at long horizons; expanding the candidate pool $k$ is a straightforward mitigation (feasibility scales linearly with $k$; e.g., $k{=}500$ yields ${\sim}52$ feasible candidates at the strictest configuration). (4)~Sequential ignorability is assumed; we provide a formal sensitivity analysis under the Rosenbaum sensitivity model (Theorem~\ref{thm:sensitivity}, Appendix~\ref{app:sensitivity}) showing that the RA filter's safety guarantee degrades gracefully under bounded hidden confounding, with breakdown points $\Gamma_{\max} = 2.1$--$7.5$ depending on planning horizon. (5)~On Cancer, model-predicted trajectories are too compressed to discriminate safety violations (Section~\ref{sec:ablations}); the safety improvements in Table~\ref{tab:gamma_sweep} depend on oracle trajectory access. On MIMIC, where predictions span the constraint boundary, model-based filtering is effective---but closing the oracle gap on synthetic data remains open. We explore conformal safety certificates (Appendix~\ref{app:conformal}) as a path toward distribution-free coverage guarantees: conformal coverage is empirically valid on both datasets, but current model accuracy falls $\sim$10\% short of the threshold for useful certification on MIMIC, defining a concrete improvement target. To demonstrate cross-domain generalization, we evaluate on a glucose-insulin simulator (Appendix~\ref{app:glucose}), confirming that RA filtering transfers directly to a domain with continuous univariate treatment, different safety semantics, and clinically defined constraint ranges. Future work includes trajectory calibration methods to close the conformal gap, multi-outcome targets, and adaptive threshold selection.

\section{Full Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:ranking_robustness}}
\label{app:proofs}

\begin{proof}

\textbf{Part (a): RA ranking preservation.}

Define the true RA probability under $p_\theta$:
\[
P(\ba) = \E_{p_\theta}\!\left[\ind\{Y_{t+\tau} \in \cT\} \prod_{s=t}^{t+\tau} \ind\{Y_s \in \cS\} \;\middle|\; \bH_t, \ba\right].
\]
Let $P_{q}(\ba)$ denote the same quantity under the variational distribution $q_\phi$. Since $f(\mathbf{Y}) := \ind\{Y_{t+\tau} \in \cT\} \prod_s \ind\{Y_s \in \cS\} \in [0, 1]$, the variational characterization of total variation gives:
\[
|P(\ba) - P_q(\ba)| = |\E_{p_\theta}[f] - \E_{q_\phi}[f]| \leq \TV(p_\theta, q_\phi) \cdot \|f\|_\infty \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}.
\]

Next, the soft-indicator approximation. Let $\hat{P}(\ba) = \E_{q_\phi}[g_\cT(Y_{t+\tau}) \prod_s g_\cS(Y_s)]$. Since $|g_\cT(y) - \ind_\cT(y)| \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}$ and $|g_\cS(y) - \ind_\cS(y)| \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}$ pointwise, and for products of $n$ terms bounded in $[0,1]$ with pointwise error $\leq \varepsilon$:
\[
\left|\prod_{i=1}^{n} g_i - \prod_{i=1}^{n} h_i\right| \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} |g_i - h_i| \leq n \cdot \varepsilon,
\]
(by telescoping and the bound $|\prod_{j>i} g_j| \leq 1$), we get $|P_q(\ba) - \hat{P}(\ba)| \leq (\tau + 1) \varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}$.

Combining by triangle inequality:
\[
|\hat{P}(\ba) - P(\ba)| \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} + (\tau + 1)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}.
\]

For two sequences $\ba_1, \ba_2$:
\begin{align}
|\hat{P}(\ba_1) - \hat{P}(\ba_2)| &\geq |P(\ba_1) - P(\ba_2)| - |\hat{P}(\ba_1) - P(\ba_1)| - |\hat{P}(\ba_2) - P(\ba_2)| \\
&\geq |P(\ba_1) - P(\ba_2)| - 2[\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} + (\tau+1)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}].
\end{align}
If $|P(\ba_1) - P(\ba_2)| > 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} + 2(\tau+1)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}$, then $|\hat{P}(\ba_1) - \hat{P}(\ba_2)| > 0$ and the ranking sign is preserved. \qed

\medskip

\textbf{Part (b): Point-target ranking threshold.}

For point-target ranking, define $d(\ba) = \E_{p_\theta}[\|Y_{t+\tau}[\ba] - Y^*\|^2]$. The function $f(Y) = \|Y - Y^*\|^2$ takes values in $[0, C_d]$ where $C_d = \sup_{\ba} \|Y_{t+\tau}[\ba] - Y^*\|^2_{L^\infty(p_\theta)}$ is the essential supremum over the model's outcome support. By the variational characterization of total variation applied to $f/C_d \in [0,1]$:
\[
|d(\ba) - d_q(\ba)| = |\E_{p_\theta}[f] - \E_{q_\phi}[f]| \leq \TV(p_\theta, q_\phi) \cdot C_d \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} \cdot C_d.
\]
The same pairwise argument as Part (a) gives ranking preservation when:
\[
|d(\ba_1) - d(\ba_2)| > 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} \cdot C_d.
\]
Since $C_d$ scales with the squared diameter of the outcome support (e.g., $C_d = V_{\max}^2$ for cancer volume $\in [0, V_{\max}]$), this threshold can be much larger than the RA threshold of $2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$---precisely the clinical regime where outcomes have high spread. \qed

\medskip

\textbf{Part (c): Strict improvement via boundary mass decomposition.}

Decompose the outcome space into three regions relative to $\cT$: the \emph{interior} $\cT^\circ_\epsilon$ (distance $> \epsilon$ from $\partial \cT$), the \emph{exterior} $(\R^d \setminus \cT)_\epsilon$ (distance $> \epsilon$ from $\partial \cT$), and the \emph{boundary strip} $\partial_\epsilon \cT$ (within $\epsilon$ of $\partial \cT$). When the boundary mass $\beta = \sup_\ba p_\theta(Y_{t+\tau}[\ba] \in \partial_\epsilon \cT) \ll 1$, most outcomes are unambiguously in or out of $\cT$.

For pairs where $\ba_1$ is feasible and $\ba_2$ is not, and both have low boundary mass:
\begin{itemize}
    \item RA margin: $m_{\mathrm{RA}} = |P(\ba_1) - P(\ba_2)| \geq (1 - \beta) - \beta = 1 - 2\beta \approx 1$.
    \item ELBO margin: the sequences may have nearly identical expected distances $d(\ba_1) \approx d(\ba_2)$ if their outcomes are on opposite sides of $\partial \cT$ at similar distances from $Y^*$, giving $m_{\mathrm{ELBO}} \approx 0$.
\end{itemize}
Thus $m_{\mathrm{RA}} \gg m_{\mathrm{ELBO}}/C_d$ when boundary mass is small. \qed
\end{proof}


\section{Justification of $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ Proxy}
\label{app:eps_proxy}

In Section~\ref{sec:ablation_eps} we estimate $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ using rank mean absolute error (rank MAE) between ELBO-predicted and ground-truth loss rankings. Here we formalize the connection between rank MAE and total variation distance, showing that our proxy yields a conservative (upper) bound on the ranking-relevant component of $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$.

\paragraph{Setup.} For a patient with $k$ candidate sequences $\ba_1, \ldots, \ba_k$, let $\sigma^*$ denote the ground-truth ranking (by true loss) and $\hat{\sigma}$ the ELBO ranking (by model loss). The rank MAE is:
\[
\mathrm{rankMAE} = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} |\hat{\sigma}(i) - \sigma^*(i)|.
\]

\paragraph{Connection to ranking displacement.} The rank MAE is the $\ell_1$ displacement between permutations, known as \emph{Spearman's footrule} $D$~\citep{diaconis1977spearman}. By the Diaconis--Graham inequality, the footrule satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dg_inequality}
K_\tau \;\leq\; D \;\leq\; 2K_\tau,
\end{equation}
where $K_\tau$ is the number of pairwise inversions (Kendall tau distance). Thus rank MAE and pairwise inversion rate are equivalent up to a factor of~2.

\paragraph{From inversions to TV.} For any pair $(\ba_i, \ba_j)$, Theorem~\ref{thm:ranking_robustness}(a) shows the pairwise ELBO ranking is preserved when the true probability margin exceeds $2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$. Contrapositively, an inversion implies
\[
|P(\ba_i) - P(\ba_j)| \leq 2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}} + 2(\tau+1)\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}}(\kappa).
\]
For hard filtering ($\kappa \geq 5$, $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{soft}} \approx 0$), the inversion rate across all candidate pairs provides a lower bound on the fraction of pairs whose probability margins are within $2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ of zero. The rank MAE thus captures the \emph{practical consequence} of the variational gap: how much the ranking is displaced.

\paragraph{Conservative bound.} We use rank MAE directly as $\hat{\varepsilon}_{\mathrm{VI}}$ because:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Rank MAE $\in [0, 1]$ after normalization by $(k-1)/2$, matching the range of TV distance.
    \item Rank MAE captures all sources of ranking error (variational gap, finite candidate pool, model misspecification), not just TV. It is therefore an \emph{upper bound} on the TV-induced ranking displacement.
    \item The bound is non-vacuous: at $\gamma = 4$, $\hat{\varepsilon}_{\mathrm{VI}} = 0.114$, so the margin condition $|P(\ba_1) - P(\ba_2)| > 2 \times 0.114 = 0.228$ is easily satisfied for feasibility-differing pairs (where $|P| = 1$).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Limitations.} This proxy conflates model error (which TV captures) with other ranking distortions (e.g., finite-sample effects, non-identifiability). A tighter bound would require direct estimation of $\TV(p_\theta, q_\phi)$ via density ratio estimation~\citep{sugiyama2012density}, which is intractable for the high-dimensional trajectory distributions in our setting. Our rank MAE proxy is a \emph{practical diagnostic} rather than a formal statistical bound; we present it transparently as such.


\section{Extended Related Work}
\label{app:extended_related_work}

We provide a comprehensive survey of the literatures that intersect with our contribution: counterfactual outcome estimation, variational methods for causal inference, dynamic treatment regimes, safe sequential decision-making, and reach-avoid control. Below we discuss each in detail and clarify the positioning of our work.

\subsection{Counterfactual Outcome Estimation Over Time}

Estimating individualized treatment effects from observational data is a rapidly growing area at the intersection of causal inference and deep learning. Foundational work by \citet{shalit2017estimating} established generalization bounds for individual treatment effect estimation via balanced representations, showing that controlling distributional distance between treated and control groups is key to reducing estimation error. This line was extended to generative modeling: CEVAE~\citep{louizos2017cevae} used variational autoencoders to simultaneously infer hidden confounders and causal effects, while GANITE~\citep{yoon2018ganite} employed generative adversarial networks to capture uncertainty in counterfactual distributions. \citet{alaa2017bayesian} took a Bayesian nonparametric approach with multi-task Gaussian processes for individualized treatment effects.

The \emph{longitudinal} setting---where treatments are administered sequentially and outcomes evolve over time---poses additional challenges from time-varying confounding. Marginal structural models~\citep{robins2000marginal} use inverse probability weighting to handle this; CRN~\citep{bica2020crn} learns balanced representations adversarially; RMSN~\citep{lim2018forecasting} combines RNNs with stabilized weights; and G-Net~\citep{li2021gnet} implements g-computation with recurrent networks. The Causal Transformer~\citep{melnychuk2022causal} adapts attention-based architectures for this setting, while ACTIN~\citep{seedat2022actin} models continuous-time treatment effects via neural controlled differential equations. SyncTwin~\citep{qian2021synctwin} constructs synthetic control twins from longitudinal pre-treatment data to estimate effects at the individual level.

Addressing hidden confounding in sequential settings, the Time Series Deconfounder~\citep{bica2020tsd} leverages factor models over treatment assignments to infer substitutes for unobserved confounders, while \citet{frauen2023sharp} derive sharp sensitivity bounds applicable to time-varying treatments under generalized marginal sensitivity models. Recent work continues to advance this frontier: Causal CPC~\citep{kim2024causalcpc} incorporates contrastive predictive coding into counterfactual regression, and \citet{wu2024counterfactual} propose conditional generative models (diffusion, CVAE) for high-dimensional counterfactual outcomes under time-varying treatments.

All of these methods estimate outcomes for \emph{given} treatment sequences but do not optimize over them. VCIP~\citep{melnychuk2025vcip} bridges this gap by selecting intervention sequences via ELBO minimization toward a point target. Our work keeps the VCIP estimation infrastructure intact and adds a safety-aware selection layer---the first to incorporate \emph{path constraints} into variational counterfactual planning.

\subsection{Variational and Generative Models for Causal Inference}

Variational autoencoders~\citep{kingma2014vae} have become a natural backbone for causal inference under latent confounding. CEVAE~\citep{louizos2017cevae} pioneered this by combining a VAE with the causal graph structure, using proxy variables to infer hidden confounders. Deep Structural Causal Models~\citep{pawlowski2020deep} extended this to full Pearl-style counterfactual reasoning by learning invertible mechanisms with normalizing flows, enabling all three levels of the causal hierarchy (association, intervention, counterfactual). \citet{zhang2021treatment} explicitly disentangled instrumental, confounding, and adjustment latent factors for treatment effect estimation.

Deep end-to-end causal inference (DECI;~\citealt{geffner2022deep}) jointly learns causal structure and parameters with a variational framework. Variational Causal Inference~\citep[VCI;][]{wu2025vci} derives an ELBO for individual-level counterfactual likelihoods in a static setting and introduces latent disentanglement via $D_{\KL}[q(z|y,t,x) \| q(z|y',t',x)]$. We draw on VCI's insight that latent action-sensitivity is a diagnostic for counterfactual model quality (Appendix~\ref{sec:e4_diagnostic}), adapting it to the sequential setting. Our diagnostic reveals that VCIP's latent space is nearly action-invariant ($D_{\KL} \approx 10^{-5}$), with action-outcome coupling residing entirely in the decoder---a structural property that explains why RA filtering operates effectively as a post-hoc method without model modification.

\subsection{Dynamic Treatment Regimes}

The DTR literature~\citep{murphy2003optimal, chakraborty2014dynamic, hernan2020causal} formalizes sequential treatment decisions under the potential outcomes framework. Classical methods include Q-learning, A-learning, and outcome-weighted learning for estimating optimal decision rules mapping patient states to treatment actions. \citet{nie2021learning} address the ``when-to-treat'' problem with advantage doubly robust estimators and welfare regret bounds under sequential ignorability, while batch policy learning methods~\citep{le2019batch} handle constraints in offline settings with finite samples.

Most DTR methods learn \emph{decision rules} (state $\to$ action mappings) that optimize expected outcomes over the full trajectory. In contrast, VCIP and our extension operate in a \emph{counterfactual planning} mode: given a specific patient history, they evaluate and rank complete intervention sequences. The reach-avoid formulation adds the constraint that trajectories must remain safe---a concern shared with constrained DTR work but addressed here through a distinct mechanism. Where constrained DTR methods embed safety into the policy learning objective (requiring retraining when constraints change), our post-hoc filtering approach is decoupled from the base model: constraints can be modified at deployment time without any retraining.

\subsection{Safe and Constrained Sequential Decision-Making}

Safety in sequential decision-making has been approached from multiple angles. The constrained MDP framework~\citep{altman1999constrained} provides the theoretical foundation, where the agent maximizes reward subject to expected cost constraints. Constrained Policy Optimization~\citep[CPO;][]{achiam2017constrained} introduced the first general-purpose policy search algorithm for constrained RL with near-constraint satisfaction guarantees at each iteration, using trust region methods. Reward Constrained Policy Optimization~\citep[RCPO;][]{tessler2019rcpo} takes a Lagrangian approach with multi-timescale updates, while CUP~\citep{yang2022cup} provides tighter constraint satisfaction via constrained update projections.

Lyapunov-based methods~\citep{chow2018safe} provide safety guarantees through stability certificates, and safety layers~\citep{dalal2018safe} project unsafe actions onto a safe set in real time. The comprehensive survey by \citet{garcia2015comprehensive} categorizes safe RL approaches into those modifying the optimization criterion, the exploration process, or both.

The concept of \emph{shielding}~\citep{alshiekh2018shielding}---a post-hoc safety monitor that overrides unsafe actions proposed by an RL agent---is the closest conceptual analogue to our approach. Where a shield intercepts individual actions, our RA filter intercepts entire candidate treatment sequences, rejecting those that violate reach-avoid feasibility. A particularly relevant recent direction is \emph{offline} safe RL, which learns safe policies from fixed datasets without environment interaction---closely paralleling our offline planning setting. FISOR~\citep{zheng2024fisor} uses Hamilton-Jacobi reachability within a diffusion model for safe offline policy learning with hard constraints, achieving zero-violation performance. This is conceptually related to our approach: both use reachability concepts for safety, but FISOR learns a policy while we filter pre-computed candidate sequences.

Chance-constrained optimization~\citep{nemirovski2006convex, calafiore2006scenario} provides a complementary perspective: constraints of the form $\Pr(g(x,\xi) \leq 0) \geq 1-\alpha$ require that safety holds with high probability over uncertain parameters $\xi$. Our feasibility check (Eq.~\ref{eq:ra_selection}) can be viewed as an empirical chance constraint, where feasibility over $k$ sampled trajectories approximates a population-level safety guarantee. Robust MDPs~\citep{iyengar2005robust, nilim2005robust} address model uncertainty by optimizing worst-case performance over an ambiguity set of transition models---a setting related to our Theorem~\ref{thm:ranking_robustness}, which bounds the effect of distributional mismatch (variational gap) on ranking.

Our setting differs fundamentally from online safe RL in that we have no opportunity for environment interaction or policy correction. We select among candidate plans evaluated on a learned model, making a \emph{filter-based} approach natural rather than constrained policy optimization. This is both a limitation (we cannot explore beyond the candidate set) and a strength (no risk of unsafe exploration, no retraining required, and the filter is model-agnostic).

\subsection{Reach-Avoid Analysis and Control}

The reach-avoid problem---steering a dynamical system into a target set while avoiding unsafe regions---has deep roots in control theory. The Hamilton-Jacobi (HJ) formulation~\citep{mitchell2005reach} computes backward reachable sets by solving a PDE, providing formal safety guarantees but suffering from exponential computational cost in the state dimension. \citet{fisac2015reach} extended this to time-varying dynamics, targets, and constraints. \citet{altschuler2023reach} offered an alternative via sum-of-squares optimization for polynomial systems.

DeepReach~\citep{bansal2021deepreach} bridges the gap between classical HJ reachability and modern deep learning, using sinusoidal neural networks to approximate value functions for high-dimensional reachability problems, with computational cost scaling with the complexity of the reachable tube rather than grid resolution. ISAACS~\citep{hsu2023isaacs} combines reach-avoid games with actor-critic methods for adversarial safety.

We import the reach-avoid concept into the variational counterfactual planning framework, but with a key simplification: rather than solving a continuous reachability PDE, we perform a \emph{discrete feasibility check} over a finite set of candidate treatment sequences. Each sequence's trajectory is evaluated against the target and safety sets using soft indicator functions, converting the reach-avoid problem from continuous optimal control to combinatorial filtering. This discrete formulation is computationally trivial (linear in the number of candidates and horizon length) and avoids the curse of dimensionality inherent in HJ methods, at the cost of being limited to the candidate set generated by the base planner.

\subsection{Safety in Clinical AI and Treatment Optimization}

AI-driven treatment optimization in clinical settings faces unique safety challenges. The AI Clinician~\citep{komorowski2018ai} pioneered the use of RL for sepsis treatment in the ICU, discretizing fluid and vasopressor dosing into 25 actions and showing that policies closer to the AI's recommendations correlated with improved 90-day survival. \citet{raghu2017deep} explored continuous state-space RL for the same problem. However, these approaches do not explicitly enforce safety constraints during planning---a limitation that has motivated subsequent work on constrained clinical RL.

In oncology, treatment planning must balance tumor control against toxicity. The cancer simulator~\citep{geng2017cancer, bica2020cancer} used in our experiments models this tradeoff: chemotherapy reduces tumor volume but accumulates toxicity. Our reach-avoid formulation directly operationalizes clinical safety constraints (e.g., tumor volume must stay below a dangerous threshold at every intermediate step, chemotherapy dosage must not exceed toxicity limits) that are implicit in clinical practice but absent from ELBO-based planning objectives.

The post-hoc filtering paradigm we propose is related to the broader concept of \emph{guardrails} in AI safety: rather than training the model to be safe, we add an external safety check on its outputs. This separation of concerns---quality from the base model, safety from the filter---has practical advantages in clinical deployment: safety thresholds can be adjusted by clinicians without requiring model retraining, and the same base model can operate under different constraint profiles for different patient populations or clinical contexts.

\section{Heterogeneity Analysis}
\label{app:heterogeneity}

We analyzed whether the Top-1 accuracy cost of RA-constrained selection is concentrated in a few ``hard'' patients or broadly distributed ($\gamma = 4$, $\tau \in \{2,4,6,8\}$).

\paragraph{Distribution of loss.} Of 100 evaluation patients, 82 are ``losers'' (experience $>5$~pp Top-1 decrease under RA constraint) and zero are ``winners'' ($>5$~pp increase). The remaining 18 are approximately neutral. The top 20\% of losers account for only 41\% of total Top-1 loss---a near-uniform distribution rather than a concentrated one.

\paragraph{Loser characterization.} Patients who lose the most Top-1 accuracy have lower average feasibility (68.2\%) compared to neutral patients (higher feasibility). These are patients whose true optimal plan happens to violate safety constraints---a fundamental limitation of hard constraints, not a correctable estimation error.

\paragraph{Implication.} Adaptive per-patient thresholds would not substantially reduce the Top-1 cost: the loss is not concentrated in identifiable subpopulations. The cost is the inherent price of the safety guarantee, distributed broadly across the patient population.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis Under Hidden Confounding}
\label{app:sensitivity}

This section provides the full proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:sensitivity} and empirical sensitivity tables for the Cancer simulator.

\subsection{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:sensitivity}}

\begin{proof}
The proof proceeds in three steps.

\textbf{Step 1: Per-step conditional TV bound.}
Fix a step $s$ and history $H_s$. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:hidden_confounding}, the unobserved confounder $U_s$ is associated with treatment $a_s$ conditionally on $H_s$. The identified outcome distribution at step $s$ integrates over $U_s$ using its marginal:
\[
P_0(Y_s \mid H_s, a_s) = \int P(Y_s \mid H_s, a_s, u)\, dP(u \mid H_s),
\]
while the true distribution conditions on the confounder-treatment association:
\[
P_\Gamma(Y_s \mid H_s, a_s) = \int P(Y_s \mid H_s, a_s, u)\, dP(u \mid H_s, a_s).
\]
By Bayes' rule, $dP(u \mid H_s, a_s) / dP(u \mid H_s) = \pi_{\mathrm{true}}(a_s \mid H_s, u) / \pi_{\mathrm{obs}}(a_s \mid H_s) \in [1/\Gamma, \Gamma]$. The total variation between two distributions with $\Gamma$-bounded density ratio satisfies (see, e.g., \citealt{rosenbaum2002observational}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:per_step_tv}
\TV(P_\Gamma(Y_s \mid H_s, a_s),\, P_0(Y_s \mid H_s, a_s)) \;\leq\; \varphi(\Gamma) := \frac{\Gamma - 1}{\Gamma + 1}.
\end{equation}
This bound holds uniformly over all histories $H_s$ and treatments $a_s$.

\textbf{Step 2: Sequential maximal coupling.}
We construct a coupling of the $\tau$-step trajectories $\bY^{(0)} \sim P_0$ and $\bY^{(\Gamma)} \sim P_\Gamma$. At each step $s = 1, \ldots, \tau$, conditional on the trajectories being coupled up to step $s-1$ (i.e., $Y^{(0)}_{1:s-1} = Y^{(\Gamma)}_{1:s-1}$), we apply a maximal coupling of $P_0(Y_s \mid H_s, a_s)$ and $P_\Gamma(Y_s \mid H_s, a_s)$. Under this coupling:
\[
\Pr(Y^{(0)}_s \neq Y^{(\Gamma)}_s \mid \text{coupled at } s{-}1) = \TV_s \leq \varphi(\Gamma).
\]
The probability that the trajectories remain coupled through all $\tau$ steps is:
\[
\Pr(\bY^{(0)} = \bY^{(\Gamma)}) \geq \prod_{s=1}^{\tau} (1 - \varphi(\Gamma)) = \left(\frac{2}{\Gamma + 1}\right)^{\!\tau}.
\]
Therefore, the total variation between the joint trajectory distributions satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sequential_tv}
\TV(P_\Gamma(\bY),\, P_0(\bY)) \;\leq\; 1 - \left(\frac{2}{\Gamma + 1}\right)^{\!\tau} =: \psi(\Gamma, \tau).
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 3: Application to reach-avoid event.}
The reach-avoid event $E(\ba)$ is a measurable function of the full trajectory $\bY$, with indicator $\ind\{E(\ba)\} \in [0,1]$. By the variational characterization of total variation:
\[
|P_\Gamma(E(\ba)) - P_0(E(\ba))| = |\E_{P_\Gamma}[\ind\{E\}] - \E_{P_0}[\ind\{E\}]| \leq \TV(P_\Gamma, P_0) \leq \psi(\Gamma, \tau).
\]
The population safety rate bound follows by linearity of expectation over patients:
$\mathrm{SR}_\Gamma = \E_x[P_\Gamma(E(\ba^*(x)))] \geq \E_x[P_0(E(\ba^*(x)))] - \psi(\Gamma, \tau) = \mathrm{SR}_0 - \psi(\Gamma, \tau)$.

The breakdown point Eq.~\eqref{eq:breakdown} is obtained by solving $\mathrm{SR}_0 - \psi(\Gamma, \tau) = 0$:
\[
\left(\frac{2}{\Gamma+1}\right)^{\!\tau} = 1 - \mathrm{SR}_0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \Gamma_{\max} = \frac{2}{(1 - \mathrm{SR}_0)^{1/\tau}} - 1. \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Remark on tightness.} The sequential coupling bound $\psi(\Gamma, \tau) = 1 - (2/(\Gamma+1))^\tau$ is strictly tighter than the union bound $\tau \cdot \varphi(\Gamma) = \tau(\Gamma-1)/(\Gamma+1)$ used in the proof sketch. For example, at $\Gamma = 1.5$, $\tau = 4$: the coupling bound gives $\psi = 0.59$ while the union bound gives $0.80$. We present the tighter bound throughout.

\section{Finite-Sample Analysis}
\label{app:finite_sample}

This section provides proofs for Propositions~\ref{prop:deployment}--\ref{prop:quality}, additional finite-sample results, and empirical verification on the Cancer simulator.

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:deployment} (Deployment Safety)}

\begin{proof}
The random variables $Z_i = \ind\{E(\ba^*_{\mathrm{RA}}(x_i))\} \in \{0, 1\}$ are i.i.d.\ (since patients are drawn i.i.d.\ from $\cP$). By Hoeffding's inequality for bounded random variables:
\[
\Pr\!\left(\widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n - \mathrm{SR}_\cP > \epsilon\right) \leq \exp(-2n\epsilon^2).
\]
Setting the right-hand side equal to $\delta/2$ and solving for $\epsilon$ gives $\epsilon = \sqrt{\ln(2/\delta)/(2n)}$. The two-sided version yields Eq.~\eqref{eq:deployment_bound}.

For part~(b), when trajectories are oracle (simulator ground truth), the RA filter checks exact feasibility. If $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}(x_i) \neq \emptyset$, the selected $\ba^*$ is feasible by construction, so $Z_i = 1$. If $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}(x_i) = \emptyset$, the fallback ELBO selection may or may not be safe, giving $Z_i \leq 1$. Thus $\mathrm{SR}_\cP \geq p_F := \Pr_{x \sim \cP}(\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}(x) \neq \emptyset)$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Bernstein tightening (Remark~\ref{rem:bernstein}).} Since $Z_i \in \{0,1\}$ are Bernoulli, $\Var(Z_i) = \mathrm{SR}_\cP(1 - \mathrm{SR}_\cP)$. Bernstein's inequality~\citep{boucheron2013concentration} gives:
\[
\Pr\!\left(\widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n - \mathrm{SR}_\cP > t\right) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{nt^2}{2\sigma^2 + \frac{2}{3}t}\right),
\]
where $\sigma^2 = \mathrm{SR}_\cP(1-\mathrm{SR}_\cP)$. Setting the right-hand side to $\delta$ and solving the resulting quadratic in $t$ gives $t_B$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:bernstein_bound}). Since $\sigma^2 \leq 1/4$ with equality only at $\mathrm{SR}_\cP = 1/2$, the Bernstein bound is always at least as tight as Hoeffding and strictly tighter when $\mathrm{SR}_\cP$ is bounded away from $1/2$---precisely the regime where RA-constrained selection operates ($\widehat{\mathrm{SR}} \geq 94\%$). We use the plug-in estimate $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n(1 - \widehat{\mathrm{SR}}_n)$; for the exact coverage guarantee, the Clopper--Pearson interval~\citep{clopper1934use} (reported in Table~\ref{tab:finite_sample}) confirms nearly identical bounds.

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:quality} (Selection Quality)}

\begin{proof}
By the uniform proxy error assumption, $|\mathrm{ELBO}(\ba) - \ell(\ba)| \leq \eta$ for all $\ba$. Then:
\begin{align}
\ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{ELBO}}) &\leq \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba^*_{\mathrm{ELBO}}) + \eta \tag{proxy error on selected} \\
&\leq \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba^*_{\mathrm{oracle}}) + \eta \tag{$\ba^*_{\mathrm{ELBO}}$ minimizes ELBO over $\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}$} \\
&\leq \ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{oracle}}) + 2\eta. \tag{proxy error on oracle-best}
\end{align}
The decomposition follows by adding and subtracting $\ell(\ba^*_{\mathrm{unconstrained}})$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Feasibility Scaling}

\begin{proposition}[Minimum candidate pool size]
\label{prop:feasibility}
If $k$ candidate sequences are generated independently with per-candidate feasibility probability $p > 0$, then:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\Pr(|\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}| \geq 1) = 1 - (1-p)^k$,
    \item $\E[|\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}|] = kp$,
    \item To guarantee $\Pr(|\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}| \geq 1) \geq 1 - \delta$, it suffices to set $k \geq \lceil \ln(1/\delta) / \ln(1/(1-p)) \rceil \approx \lceil \ln(1/\delta) / p \rceil$ for small $p$.
\end{itemize}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$|\cF_{\mathrm{RA}}| \sim \mathrm{Binomial}(k, p)$. The probability of an empty feasible set is $(1-p)^k$. Setting $(1-p)^k \leq \delta$ and taking logarithms gives the result; the approximation uses $\ln(1-p) \approx -p$ for $p \ll 1$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Chance-Constrained Interpretation}

The RA-constrained selection with hard filter ($\kappa \to \infty$) solves:
\[
\min_{\ba \in \{\ba_1, \ldots, \ba_k\}} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad E(\ba) \text{ holds on the evaluated trajectory.}
\]
This is a \emph{single-scenario} approximation to the population chance-constrained program~\citep{nemirovski2006convex}:
\[
\min_{\ba} \mathrm{ELBO}(\ba) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \Pr(E(\ba)) \geq 1 - \alpha,
\]
where the probability is over trajectory randomness. For deterministic simulators (Cancer), $\Pr(E(\ba)) \in \{0, 1\}$ and the single-scenario evaluation is exact. For stochastic outcomes (MIMIC), a single model-predicted trajectory provides a noisy estimate of the chance constraint. The scenario approach~\citep{calafiore2006scenario} provides guarantees when $N$ i.i.d.\ trajectory samples are available; with $N = 1$ (our setting), the guarantee is weak, motivating the oracle evaluation paradigm for Cancer and the conformal approach (Appendix~\ref{app:conformal}) for distribution-free certificates.

\subsection{Distributionally Robust Interpretation}
\label{sec:dro_interpretation}

The RA threshold filter also admits a distributionally robust optimization (DRO) interpretation. Under Assumption~\ref{ass:vi_gap}, the true model $p_\theta$ lies within a total variation ball of radius $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ around the variational approximation $q_\phi$. The RA feasibility check $\hat{P}(\ba) \geq \delta$ (for threshold $\delta$) is equivalent to verifying:
\[
\min_{Q:\, \TV(Q, q_\phi) \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}} P_Q(E(\ba)) \;\geq\; \delta - \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}},
\]
where $P_Q(E(\ba))$ is the reach-avoid probability under distribution $Q$. This follows directly from the variational characterization of TV for $[0,1]$-valued functionals (Theorem~\ref{thm:ranking_robustness}(a)): the worst-case probability over the ambiguity set $\{Q : \TV(Q, q_\phi) \leq \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}\}$ is at most $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ below the nominal probability. Thus, the RA filter implicitly provides a DRO guarantee: any plan declared feasible at level $\delta$ by the model satisfies the true reach-avoid constraint at level $\geq \delta - \varepsilon_{\mathrm{VI}}$ for \emph{all} distributions within the TV ambiguity set. This connects the threshold-based filter to the robust optimization literature~\citep{iyengar2005robust} and provides an interpretive bridge between ranking robustness (Theorem~\ref{thm:ranking_robustness}) and classical DRO.

\section{Conformal Safety Certificates}
\label{app:conformal}

The RA threshold filter in Section~\ref{sec:method} uses model-predicted trajectories (or oracle trajectories) to determine feasibility, but provides no formal guarantee that the \emph{true} outcome will satisfy constraints. Here we investigate whether split conformal prediction~\citep{vovk2005algorithmic} can provide distribution-free safety certificates for counterfactual treatment plans.

\subsection{Framework}

Given a calibration set of $n$ patient--treatment pairs $\{(X_i, \ba_i, Y_i^{\mathrm{obs}})\}_{i=1}^n$ and a trajectory predictor $\hat{\mu}$, we compute nonconformity scores
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nonconformity}
s_i = Y_i^{\mathrm{obs}} - \hat{\mu}(X_i, \ba_i) \quad (\text{signed residual}),
\end{equation}
and the conformal quantile $\hat{q}_\alpha = \mathrm{Quantile}_{1-\alpha}\bigl(\{s_i\}_{i=1}^n\bigr)$ at level $\lceil(1-\alpha)(n+1)\rceil / n$. For a new patient with candidate treatment $\ba$, the one-sided prediction bound is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:conformal_bound}
Y_{\mathrm{true}}(\ba) \leq \hat{\mu}(X, \ba) + \hat{q}_\alpha \quad \text{with probability} \geq 1-\alpha.
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Conformal safety certificate]
A treatment plan $\ba$ is \emph{certified safe at level $\alpha$} if the upper prediction bound lies within the constraint region:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mu}(X, \ba) + \hat{q}_\alpha \leq \delta_{\cT} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{\mu}_{\max}(X, \ba) + \hat{q}_{\alpha,\cS} \leq \delta_{\cS},
\end{equation}
where $\delta_\cT, \delta_\cS$ are the target and safety thresholds, respectively.
\end{definition}

\noindent The certified set $\cF_{\mathrm{conf}} = \{\ba : \text{certified at level } \alpha\}$ guarantees: any plan in $\cF_{\mathrm{conf}}$ satisfies the true constraints with probability $\geq 1-\alpha$, requiring only exchangeability of calibration and test residuals.

\subsection{Empirical Results}

We apply conformal certificates to both datasets using calibration seeds $\{10, 101, 1010\}$ and test seeds $\{10101, 101010\}$ with $\alpha = 0.10$ (90\% coverage target).

\paragraph{Coverage is empirically valid.} On Cancer, oracle coverage (fraction of true outcomes below the conformal upper bound) is 85--91\% across all $\gamma$ and $\tau$ settings, confirming the conformal guarantee holds. On MIMIC-III with symmetric bands, empirical coverage is 87--92\%, close to the 90\% target.

\paragraph{Certification is too conservative for selection.} On Cancer with one-sided bounds, the conformal quantiles $\hat{q}_\alpha$ are small relative to the gap between model predictions and constraints. Since model predictions cluster at $0$--$2$~cm$^3$ while the target threshold is $3.0$~cm$^3$, the upper bound $\hat{\mu} + \hat{q}_\alpha$ lies well below the threshold for nearly all candidates, yielding 100\% certification---the filter certifies everything and provides no selection discrimination. On MIMIC-III with asymmetric bounds (separate upper/lower calibration), the total band width ($\approx 33$~mmHg at $\alpha = 0.10$) exceeds the target range width ($30$~mmHg), making certification infeasible for any candidate (0\% certification).

\paragraph{Accuracy gap.} The accuracy gap---the factor by which model prediction quality must improve for non-vacuous certificates---is quantifiable:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cancer:} One-sided certificates are vacuously satisfied (ratio $< 1$) because model predictions are too far from constraint boundaries. Certificates would become discriminating only if model accuracy degraded, which is the wrong direction.
\item \textbf{MIMIC:} The band/target ratio is $1.08$--$1.15$, meaning a $\sim$10\% reduction in prediction MAE (from $\sim$10~mmHg to $\sim$9~mmHg) would enable non-vacuous conformal certificates. This is tantalizingly close.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implications}

These results establish a \emph{three-regime framework} for safety in counterfactual planning:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Certified safety} (conformal certificates): provides distribution-free guarantees but requires model accuracy within the accuracy gap. Not achievable with current decoders.
\item \textbf{Optimistic safety} (RA threshold filter): uses model predictions without formal guarantees. Effective on MIMIC where predictions span the constraint boundary; ineffective on Cancer where predictions are compressed.
\item \textbf{Oracle safety} (ground-truth RA filter): uses simulator trajectories. Upper bound on achievable safety; available only in synthetic settings.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The accuracy gap diagnostic ($\sim$10\% on MIMIC) provides a concrete model improvement target: once trajectory prediction accuracy crosses this threshold, the RA filter can be upgraded to conformal certificates with formal coverage guarantees. This motivates future work on calibrated trajectory prediction and conformal methods for sequential counterfactual outcomes~\citep{lei2021conformal}.

\subsection{Treatment Pattern Shift}

RA filtering consistently selects plans with substantially lower vasopressor rates than observed (0.00--0.07 vs.\ observed 0.07--0.22). This reflects the confounded nature of the data: patients who \emph{received} vasopressors were sicker (lower baseline DBP), and their observed vasopressor use is driven by clinical need, not by the vasopressors causing good outcomes. The model learns $\hat{\mu}(\text{less vasopressor}) \approx \text{higher predicted DBP}$, so the RA filter---which targets DBP $\in [60,90]$---preferentially selects lower-vasopressor plans. This is a known limitation of observational treatment recommendation: the confounding between treatment intensity and baseline severity is difficult to fully adjust for, even with causal models.

\subsection{Guideline Concordance}
\label{sec:guideline_concordance}

We assess concordance of RA-selected treatments with the Surviving Sepsis Campaign (SSC) 2021 guidelines for hemodynamic management, focusing on two key recommendations: (i)~vasopressor use for hypotensive patients (target MAP $\geq 65$~mmHg; our proxy: DBP $\geq 60$~mmHg), and (ii)~avoidance of unnecessary vasopressor escalation for hemodynamically stable patients.

We stratify patients by baseline observed DBP into three groups: low ($< 60$~mmHg, $n = 1{,}060$), target ($60$--$90$~mmHg, $n = 883$), and high ($> 90$~mmHg, $n = 52$).

\paragraph{Stable patients: appropriate de-escalation.} For patients already within the hemodynamic target, RA-selected plans avoid vasopressor use in 97.2\% of cases, compared with 90.4\% for ELBO and 88.6\% for observed treatments. Unnecessary vasopressor escalation occurs in only 0.1\% of RA selections vs.\ 1.7\% for ELBO. This aligns with the SSC recommendation to avoid unnecessary vasopressor escalation in stable patients.

\paragraph{Hypotensive patients: concordance depends on definition.} Under a \emph{strict} interpretation (SSC recommends vasopressor initiation for hypotensive patients), RA concordance is low: only 5.8\% of RA plans include vasopressors for low-DBP patients, vs.\ 22.6\% for ELBO and 22.1\% for observed treatment. This apparent discordance arises from the model's learned inverse relationship between vasopressor use and predicted terminal DBP: candidates with vasopressors are predicted to have terminal DBP $\approx 56.5$~mmHg (below target), while candidates without vasopressors are predicted at $\approx 61.4$~mmHg (within target). Since the RA filter selects plans with predicted DBP $\in [60,90]$, it systematically excludes vasopressor-containing candidates.

Under a \emph{pathway-aware} interpretation---where concordance requires reaching the hemodynamic target regardless of the specific treatment used---RA achieves 100\% concordance for low-DBP patients (all RA-selected plans have predicted terminal DBP $\geq 60$ by construction), compared with 92.8\% for ELBO.

\paragraph{Temporal pattern.} At longer horizons ($\tau = 5$), RA-selected plans exhibit mild front-loading of treatment intensity (fluid rate 0.26 at step 1 $\to$ 0.19 at step 5), directionally consistent with SSC's ``early aggressive resuscitation, then de-escalate'' principle, though absolute vasopressor rates remain very low throughout.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The discrepancy between strict and pathway-aware concordance reflects a fundamental tension in observational treatment recommendation: the model learns associations from confounded data where sicker patients both receive more vasopressors \emph{and} have worse outcomes, making it difficult to separate treatment effect from baseline severity. The RA filter operates on model predictions and inherits this limitation. This finding underscores the need for either (i)~stronger causal adjustment in the underlying model, or (ii)~clinician-in-the-loop review of recommended plans before deployment.

\subsection{Limitations}

This analysis has several important limitations: (1)~it uses \emph{predicted} terminal DBP to classify outcomes (the ``good-outcome'' label is model-dependent, not ground truth); (2)~step agreement is a crude metric that does not capture clinical equivalence (two treatments may be equally effective but differ in timing or dosing); (3)~the analysis is correlational---higher agreement with good-outcome patients does not prove the RA-selected plan \emph{caused} good outcomes; (4)~guideline concordance is assessed against simplified proxies of SSC 2021 recommendations, not the full clinical protocol; and (5)~without clinician evaluation, we cannot assess whether the recommended treatment sequences are clinically plausible in context. These limitations motivate the clinician evaluation proposed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\section{Second Evaluation Domain: Glucose-Insulin Simulator}
\label{app:glucose}

To demonstrate cross-domain generalization, we evaluate RA-constrained selection on a glucose-insulin dynamics simulator based on the Bergman minimal model~\citep{bergman1979quantitative}. This domain differs from the cancer simulator in several key ways: (i)~continuous single-dimensional treatment (insulin dose) rather than binary two-dimensional treatment (chemo/radio), (ii)~physiologically distinct safety semantics (hypoglycemia is immediately dangerous, while hyperglycemia is harmful over longer horizons), and (iii)~a clinically meaningful target range defined by standard glycemic control guidelines.

\subsection{Simulator Design}

The Bergman minimal model describes glucose-insulin dynamics through three coupled ODEs governing plasma glucose $G(t)$ (mg/dL), insulin action $X(t)$ (1/min), and plasma insulin $I(t)$ (mU/L):
\begin{align}
\frac{dG}{dt} &= -p_1(G - G_b) - X \cdot G + D(t)/V_g \\
\frac{dX}{dt} &= -p_2 X + p_3(I - I_b) \\
\frac{dI}{dt} &= -n(I - I_b) + u(t)/V_i
\end{align}
where $u(t)$ is the exogenous insulin dose, $D(t)$ is meal-derived glucose absorption, and $\{p_1, p_2, p_3, n, G_b, I_b, V_g, V_i\}$ are patient-specific parameters. We sample three patient types (insulin-sensitive, normal, insulin-resistant) with physiologically realistic parameter ranges.

\paragraph{Confounding mechanism.}
To create observational data with treatment-outcome confounding, we use a sigmoid-based confounded insulin policy:
$P(\text{high dose} \mid G) = \sigma(\gamma \cdot (G - 150)/30)$,
where $\gamma$ controls confounding strength. We fix $\gamma = 4$ to match the cancer experiments.

\paragraph{Data generation.}
We generate 48-hour trajectories (1 step/hour) for 1000 training, 100 validation, and 100 test patients, with realistic meal schedules (3 meals + snacks) and convert to VCIP-compatible format. For counterfactual evaluation, we generate $k = 100$ candidate insulin sequences per test patient at each prediction horizon $\tau \in \{2, 4, 6, 8\}$ hours.

\subsection{Constraint Specification}

Following standard glycemic control guidelines:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Target set} $\cT$: terminal glucose $\in [70, 180]$ mg/dL (euglycemic range)
\item \textbf{Safety set} $\cS$: all glucose values $\in [50, 250]$ mg/dL (avoids severe hypo/hyperglycemia)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results}

We train VCIP on the glucose data using the same architecture and hyperparameters as the cancer experiments (5 random seeds), then apply RA-constrained selection using model-predicted ELBO losses.

\paragraph{Key findings.}
(1)~RA filtering transfers directly to a new domain with different treatment dimensionality, outcome semantics, and constraint definitions, confirming model-agnostic applicability. (2)~The ELBO-outcome Spearman correlation ranges from $\rho = 0.24$ ($\tau = 8$) to $\rho = 0.63$ ($\tau = 4$), demonstrating that VCIP provides informative but imperfect rankings in this domain. (3)~RA improvement is largest at $\tau = 8$ (+2.0 pp), where feasibility is lowest (69.6\%) and ELBO selection is most likely to pick infeasible candidates---precisely the setting where safety filtering provides the most value. (4)~At $\tau = 6$ the improvement is zero; this occurs because the ELBO-selected candidate happens to already be feasible for all patients at this horizon. (5)~Cross-seed variance is notably low ($\leq 0.8$ pp), indicating stable performance across random initializations.
